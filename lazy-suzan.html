Lazy Suzan

SUMMARY

Lazy Suzan was a application developed over the course of a semester in an introductory human-centered design course. The project required that my team and I use interviews, competitor analysis, prototyping, and user testing to create and evaluate a application of our choice. We chose to create a food preparation and recipe organization application called "Lazy Suzan."

DESIGN METHOD

Information Collection

Competitor Analyses were conducted to identify existing applications such as Hello Fresh and Yummly which offer solutions to the problem of meal preparation organization. This and prior experience in the kitchen were used to identify tasks that cooks must complete when organizing large meals. These assumptions were checked using a survey of family and friends who enjoy cooking or do so professionally. Questions asked included, "How many times per week do you cook?", "How often do you cook from a recipe?" and "Tell us about your worst cooking experience."

Solution Development

The next step was to translate what we gleaned from our conversations with users and stakeholders and translate it into a functional prototype. To do this, our team worked with Axure, each of us taking a different feature of the app and building it out to meet our pre-defined specifications. The first prototype, which was used to guide a heuristic with a sample of non-users, is shown in Figure 3.

Gather Feedback

We used the Axure prototype to gather feedback from Human-Computer Interaction experts during a Heuristic Review. From this, we determined that more work needed to be put into our microcopy throughout the prototype, we needed to ensure our interactions were consistent across the platform, and the process for adding multiple recipes to a single meal was unclear. Using this and other feedback, we created the second iteration of our Lazy Suzan prototype.

Improve

Several improvements were made to the app prototype following our first round evaluation, including the change shown in Figure 4.

Figure 4. In the first version of the prototype, a check marked box served as the path for adding additional recipes to a meal. In the second version, "Add More Recipes" and "Create Meal" are clearly-marked buttons on the screen.

CONCLUSIONS

After implementing the changes recommended by our non-user sample, we planned another iteration of user testing. With more time, we were going to use validated surveys (NASA TLX and SUS) to understand how well our product compared to a competitor, allrecipes. We were going to use contextual inquiry to observe our users in their own kitchens, so that the only variable being manipulated was the product used to organize their recipes. To save time, we planned to use two instances of 10-minutes of simulated cooking with either application. NASA TLX and SUS scores would be taken after each 10-minute task, and participants would be debreifed at the end of the study.

CHALLENGES

  1. Teamwork

    2. As in most group projects, especially as we are learning how to cooperate and collaborate in professional ways, teamwork was a challenge. Three of the four of us had prior working relationships and fell in step fairly easy; however, we experienced some dissonance with our remaining member. They were not a heavy contributor, likely due to their level of maturity and motivation style. In this experience, I mostly just struggled on and left a negative review of that partner. However, if I could go back and relive this project, I would have taken time to layout expectations prior to the commencement of our project. Communication is key.

  2. Prototyping

    3. I love building prototypes, don't get me wrong, but the process changes immensely when you work with a team instead of independently. This project taught me a few lessons about properly allocating responsibilities and having a design standard to avoid inconsistencies in the interface and in the user experience.
