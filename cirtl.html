CIRTL

Center for the Integration of Research, Teaching, and Learning

Summary

In the last few years, several movements in America have sought to bring attention to issues of race, gender, and sexual orientation. For example, Black Lives Matter reminded us that treatment of people of color is not yet where it should be.

Academia is no different than the rest of the country. For a variety of reasons, the number of underrepresented racial minority (URM) students who choose to become professors is tiny. But, this trend is not unnoticed. The Center for the Integration of Research, Teaching, and Learning (CIRTL) continues to seek answers to this issue, retention. (For more information, visit the NSF Award site.)

CIRTL hopes to use informative workshops to improve the experiences of and increase retention for URM students. To assess any change, the CIRTL team needed to see and understand the numbers. My job was to design data visualizations which summarized data from the annual surveys and the workshops. I approached the project as though I were creating visuals for my own data set.

Design Method

Problems
  1. What data visualizations will answer the most questions for CIRTL?
  2. What level of interactivity should be included in the base data dashboard?
Information Collection

I used a combination of interviewing and research to collect information about the project. Scouring the NSF Grant, I learned that the project focused on URM student interest in faculty careers. I also discovered that the main areas of interest related to faculty careers and teacher or researcher self-efficacy. I prioritized the variables that appeared in the NSF Grant as I generated ideas.

After I gathered initial information from the NSF Grant, I approached the team with requirements questions. I wanted to know how the team would know if a workshop intervention had worked. I also needed to determine the audience. (Would the data dashboards stay internal, or would university administrators see them?) If I could do this again, I would have made some very rough mock-ups, even at such an early point in the process. Despite not being as prepared as I could have been, I did have a better idea of where I was headed.

Idea Generation

First, I printed copies of the project's survey questions. I then wrote scale names, question categories, etc. on post-it notes and arranged them into a sort of affinity diagram. Physically grouping the variables aided my discovery of potential relationships in the data. Below is a picture of the affinity diagram for the graduate student annual survey.

Solution Development

Because I was new to Tableau and needed to work fast and without data, I chose to reference Tableau and mock-up dashboards. First, I sketched a sample of potential graphs that could be included in a prototype.

Gather Feedback After gaining some initial feedback regarding the effectiveness of the graphs, I created higher fidelity mock-ups in PowerPoint. I could have used Axure or Balsamiq to make the prototypes, but I chose PowerPoint to streamline the future presentation of the visualizations

.

These PowerPoint decks were used to solidify design requirements with the members of CIRTL. I wanted to rapidly prototype my ideas in a way that delineated the idea without further fostering a connection between me and my "masterpieces."

 

Because I was working in PowerPoint, interactivity was easier explained than shown. Needing only to know what relationships they wanted to explore, I didn't want the stakeholders to get held-up in how the interaction looked.

One surprising bit of feedback I received was about the exclusion of the gender demographics variable. I had assumed that since it was not the goal of the project to learn about the experiences of people with different genders, this variable did not have a place in the visualizations. The stakeholders wanted to identify all  barriers to retention in faculty career paths. Therefore, if any gender-related issues were present, they wanted to be able to address them.

Additionally, the team pointed out that I had included others when a question had a text-response to follow the selection of the other response. While I had assumed that the individual detail would not be important and wanted to simplify data organization, the stakeholders wanted to be able to address all data, not just the "aggregates."

Improve

Due to a lapse in funding, I did not improve the visualizations beyond the first draft. From this point, the plan had been to get the data frame to talk to Tableau, then to collect data, and then to implement the gathered design requirements. If I had continued on the project, I would have at least sketched the ideas I had for improving each dashboard. Instead, I wrote out the ideas I had for each dashboard, listed the action items, and consolidated my notes. I enclosed all that information in an email to the team so that when they were ready, they could use my knowledge to speed up their process.

Challenges

  1. Learning to be a designer

    2. Going into this project, I had no formal experience as a designer. From my perspective, this was a disadvantage, for if I had a better appreciation for design standards and process, I might have been able to deliver a more put-together final product. Instead, when I left at the end of the summer, I had a list of requirements and some prototype visualizations.

  2. Eliciting design requirements from stakeholders

    3. As I was a naïve UX designer, I completely underestimated the effort I would need to put into eliciting design requirements from the CIRTL members. I eventually did gain some feedback, but I had to develop a product before I got any comments. As I move forward, I will rely less on language and more on prototypes and sketches.

  3. Learning to embrace failure

    4. I had no idea how difficult it was to offer designs up for true critique. I had taken a few art classes which included critiques, but they had been relatively tame. I quickly learned that I was designing for failure, rather than for perfection. I had to take chances and make best-guesses if I wanted to learn anything from my designs.
